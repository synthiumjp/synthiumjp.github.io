Research

My work sits at the intersection of developmental cognitive science and computational modelling. I use autoregressive and masked language models trained on child-directed speech as experimental tools to probe what distributional learning can and cannot achieve — testing formal theories of word learning, inductive bias, and referential pragmatics against computational boundary conditions.

A secondary thread applies psychometric and signal-detection methodology to the evaluation of AI systems, asking whether standard cognitive science measurement frameworks can characterise model behaviour more precisely than existing benchmarks.

Recent Work

  • Exemplar Retrieval Without Overhypothesis Induction: Limits of Distributional Sequence Learning in Early Word Learning Under review

    Cognition  ·  2026

    Pre-registered test of whether autoregressive transformers trained on synthetic child-scale corpora induce overhypotheses (second-order generalisations about category structure). Models achieve perfect first-order exemplar retrieval but remain at chance on second-order generalisation across 120 runs and three model sizes. Feature-swap diagnostics reveal template matching rather than structured abstraction as the operative mechanism.

    Pre-registration & code available on acceptance

  • Repetition Without Exclusivity: Scale Sensitivity of Referential Mechanisms in Child-Scale Language Models Preprint

    arXiv:2603.13696  ·  TACL (forthcoming)  ·  2026

    First systematic evaluation of mutual exclusivity in text-only language models trained on child-directed speech. Across 45 GPT-2-architecture models spanning a 12× parameter range, all models show robust repetition priming rather than ME. Priming attenuates with better language modelling but never crosses zero. A context-dependence diagnostic rules out embedding artefacts as an alternative account.

    arXiv OSF pre-registration Code

  • Do Structural Priors Help Neural Language Models Learn Grammar? Evidence from Child-Scale Data Submitted

    1st Workshop on Computational Developmental Linguistics  ·  ACL 2026  ·  2026

    Pre-registered evaluation of a neurosymbolic architecture combining BabyBERTa with a differentiable PCFG auxiliary loss derived from Minimalist Grammar. Structural priors produce phenomenon-specific effects: improving filler-gap dependencies by 9–13 pp (d = 2.41–2.82) while damaging locally-cued phenomena regardless of whether the grammar is real or random.

    Pre-registration & code available on acceptance

  • LLMs as Signal Detectors: Sensitivity, Bias, and the Temperature-Criterion Analogy Preprint

    arXiv:2603.14893  ·  2026

    Applying SDT frameworks from psychophysics to characterise sensitivity and bias in LLM classification behaviour. Pre-registered.

    arXiv OSF pre-registration
